Measuring Air Pollution in Karnataka with Data from Monitoring Stations and Low Cost Sensors

MANU KONCHADY

Bengaluru:  Air quality in New Delhi and North India is often cited in the media. However, air pollution is not limited to North India alone. Cities and towns across Karnataka, Tamil Nadu, Kerala, Telangana, and Andhra Pradesh are exposed to air pollution from vehicular emissions, construction activity, and industrial growth.

The three main sources for air pollution data are - Public monitoring stations that are maintained by the government, private organizations that collect data for mobile apps like IQAir and AQI, and data collected by individuals not associated with any organization.   

The official source for air pollution data is from public monitoring stations maintained by the Central Pollution Control Board (CPCB) and State Pollution Control Boards. Bengaluru, a city spread over 720 square kilometers, has about 12 monitoring stations or one station for 60 square kilometers. The large gaps in the coverage of air pollution measurements make it hard to identify the variation in concentrations of pollutants at a lower resolution.

New Delhi with an area about twice the area of Bengaluru collects data from about 50 monitoring stations.  A study in 2019 identified the low density of air quality monitors per million in India compared to the ratio in other countries and  supported the use of measurements from low cost sensors to supplement the data from public monitoring stations.

Another study recommended at least 41 air quality monitoring stations in Bengaluru. Satellite data does provide concentrations of pollutants at a high resolution of about 1 square kilometer but is not a substitute for ground monitoring stations.

The region in the Western Ghats comprising about 7500 square kilometers within Udupi, Mangalore, Hassan, and Chikkamagalur had four monitoring stations, of which two were inactive (as of February 2026). The limited data from this region makes it difficult to identify long term changes in air pollution.

Accurate data collection and analysis is the first and critical step in solving the air pollution problem. Yet, the current budget for pollution control in 2026-27 was reduced by about 15%.  A study in 2018 (page 3) of air pollution in Bengaluru cited the lack of reliable and sufficient monitoring stations as a deterrent to understand the severity of the crisis.

Given the high cost of land acquisition in Bengaluru and hardware for a monitoring station, it is unlikely that the density of monitors will increase to match the recommended  density. One solution is to supplement the data collected by the central and state pollution boards with data from portable air quality monitors owned by citizens.

After proper calibration, the accuracy of some low-cost monitors can approach that of official monitoring stations for certain pollutants such as PM2.5. Tests of low cost air quality monitors have shown that accuracy is not necessarily proportional to the cost of the monitor.  Current air quality monitors range from industrial models costing a lakh INR or more to portable models costing several thousand INR.  

In this article, the measurements from a portable Temtop M2000 model with a reported accuracy of 80% were used to measure air pollution in Bengaluru city, the coast of Karnataka, and the Western Ghats. The measurements were captured using the portable monitor mounted on the top tube of a bicycle, over a distance of about 500 kilometers.

Key Observations from Data Collected with a Low Cost Sensor

The observations below from a Temtop monitor are likely to be of low precision compared to the regulator grade monitor at an official station. The collected data may also be sensitive to humidity and temperature. Despite issues with precision, data from low cost and affordable sensors empower the average citizen to report the severity of air pollution at the local level. The results can be used to persuade the government to invest more in monitoring air pollution.  

  • The data collected from low cost sensors offers a perspective of air pollution at the road level that is not available from the data collected at a monitoring station.
  • The exposure to pollution on different routes to the same location depends on the route and some routes have a slightly lower exposure than other routes.
  • The air quality close to a monitoring station can vary and is not related to the distance from the station. The AQI on the road within a few 100 meters from the monitoring station can be significantly higher than the AQI measured at the monitoring station.
  • Deep in the Kudremukh National Forest, the Air Quality Index (AQI) was about one tenth the AQI at the university town of Manipal, about 40 kilometers away.
  • The AQI reduces (improves) by about 10 for every kilometer further away from Manipal, up to a distance of 20 kilometers.
  • The Kudremukh National Forest is a buffer zone (airshed) with better air quality between more populated and polluted areas.
  • There is practically no difference in the AQI between a town located at the base of the Western Ghats at an elevation of 100 meters and another town located on the Deccan plateau at an elevation of 1000 meters.

Types of Air Pollutants

The Central Pollution Control Board of the Government of India tracks  eight air pollutants – particulate matter smaller than 2.5 micrometers (PM2.5), particulate matter between 2.5 and 10 micrometers (PM10), nitrogen dioxide, sulfur dioxide, carbon monoxide, ozone, ammonia, and lead. The particulate matter pollutants, PM2.5 and PM10, are extremely small yet visible only when concentrations are large.

The remaining six air pollutants are not visible and occur as gases that are inhaled. Although these gases exist in the form of much smaller molecules that are measured in nanometers. Unlike particulate matter, gaseous pollutants do not physically accumulate in lung tissue. However, they can react chemically with respiratory tissues leading to breathing difficulties, cough, and other lung ailments.

Particulate matter, PM2.5 and PM10, exist in the form of solids and liquids and are considered more harmful than gaseous pollutants since they get trapped in our lungs and are not exhaled. PM10 particles are larger and tend to be trapped in the upper air tract including the nose, mouth, and throat.

Air Pollution Data from Monitoring Stations

The Central Pollution Control Board publishes air quality data that can be downloaded for cities in India, organized by monitoring station and frequency (hourly or daily) of data collection.  The map below shows the AQI data collected for the year 2025 from 11 monitoring stations located in Bengaluru city. The first four categories for AQI are defined as Good (green), Satisfactory (yellow), Moderate (orange), and Poor (red)  by the Ministry of Earth Science, Government of India.

        

The map shows the percentage of AQI readings by category in a bar for each of the 11 stations and the 12 months. Each of the 11 stations show different percentages for the four categories from the cumulative AQI data. Out of the 11 stations, about 3 stations have 50% or more AQI readings in the green zone.

Similarly, the cumulative AQI data from all 11 stations for each month varies, with the best air quality during the months from May through September. The worst months for AQI are from November through January. This seasonal variation in AQI aligns with wind patterns during the monsoon. The stronger winds during the monsoon season coincide with periods of better air quality.

The average cumulative PM 2.5 concentrations by the hour of day also follow a predicted pattern. The early morning and late night hours tend to have the worst air quality of the day. One explanation is that the cooler air early and late in the day is suitable for the build up of air pollution.

The quality of the data collected from monitoring stations is presumed to be accurate since the Government of India has specific technical guidelines for the type of hardware at an official monitoring station. Still, there are anomalies such as the AQI data from the monitor at the Bengaluru Railway Station.

On several days the same identical AQI was reported hour after hour at the Railway monitoring station, which would appear unrealistic. Notwithstanding the occasional discrepancy, data from monitoring stations is a baseline to collect more detailed information about air pollution.

Low Cost Sensors to Monitor Air Quality

One of the drawbacks of depending on data from a limited number of monitoring stations is that air pollution is rarely evenly distributed across several square kilometers in a city. Data from a monitoring station will not capture the differences in air pollution at the street or neighbourhood level.

The process of collecting and integrating data from low cost sensors (page 16)  with the data from monitoring stations to assess the variation in air pollution is documented.  

A case study in 2019 pointed out that the air quality monitor density of 0.14 monitors per million in India was well below the density in several other countries.  The study also mentioned that small scale variability in air pollution levels in densely populated urban areas would not be identified. In 2023, only  12% of Indian cities and towns had monitoring stations.

One way to address this gap in air quality measurement is to supplement the data collected from public monitor stations with data collected from low cost but reasonably accurate sensors.   A  good sensor must also be precise, i.e. give consistent readings for the same environment.

A simple way to verify the accuracy of a low cost sensor is through calibration. Comparing the over 100 different models of air quality monitors that are sold online is not feasible for a potential buyer. The Council of Scientific & Industrial Research was tasked with certifying air quality monitors, however this information was not found on the Web.

Monitoring Air Quality with a Low Cost Sensor on a Bicycle

The air pollution data collected from monitoring stations and devices mounted on rooftops of houses can be supplemented with data from monitors mounted on a bicycle. Air pollution data collected at the street level reveals the degree of exposure to pollution for commuters that cannot be collected from static monitors.

The idea of using a bicycle to collect air quality data has been implemented in Germany, Netherlands, Spain and the US. The main benefit was the collection of data at a higher spatial resolution in cities that are fairly densely populated.

The results from a bicycle based monitor also show how pollution can vary on a street at intervals of a few hundred meters. The results here show how air quality collected from a monitor on a bicycle, varies within Bengaluru city, the coast of Karnataka, and the Western Ghats.

Three Routes to the Center of Bengaluru

In 2025, Bengaluru was ranked the second most congested city in the world. The routes suggested by mobile apps like Google Maps consider data from multiple sources to find an optimal route that saves time. Exposure to air pollution is not a consideration when computing an optimal route.

Three different routes to the central business district in Bengaluru from the same location show how air quality exposure varies depending on the route. The shortest route on main roads has the highest air pollution exposure compared to the two other routes. Pollution exposure is fairly high in all three routes and a “green” route is not obvious.

Air Quality near a Monitoring Station in Bengaluru

The monitoring station at Hebbal, Bengaluru is located a few 100 meters from a main road. The Central Pollution Control Board does not specify a radius from the monitor for which readings will be valid. In an urban area where pollution is not uniform, there is no scientific argument that states how far from the monitor a reading will be valid.

The map shows that the AQI (in red) on the busy road close to the monitoring station in Hebbal is much larger than the AQI of 200 at the monitoring station. A few locations on the road have a lower AQI (in blue) and the remaining locations have an AQI that is slightly above the AQI of the monitoring station.

Air Quality over 20 Kilometers near the Coast of Karnataka

Manipal, a university town in the coast of Karnataka with a population of over 50,000, is fairly densely populated compared to the rest of its district, Udupi. As a result the traffic and corresponding level of pollution is higher compared to the surrounding areas.

The AQI of 280 is the highest at the center of Manipal where several roads intersect at a junction. The black line shows how AQI decreases with increasing distance from Manipal. The improvement in AQI is not uniformly linear but steadily decreases with distance. At about 20 kilometers from the center of Manipal, AQI drops by 200 from 280 to 80 which is roughly an improvement of 10 in the AQI for every kilometer away from the  main source of pollution.

Air Quality over 60 Kilometers from the Coast of Karnataka

The Kudremukh National Forest begins at about 30 kilometers away from Manipal and is spread over about 600 square kilometers. Deep in the forest, the AQI is significantly lower with the lowest AQI at 43. The term “airshed” is used to describe regions with relatively uniform levels of air pollution. The Kudremukh National Forest is an airshed with moderate pollution between two airsheds of unhealthy pollution levels.

The benefits of the improved AQI from the forest extend over 20 kilometers beyond the edge of the forest. East of the forest, the AQI becomes worse near the town of Kalasa. This is possibly due to the higher burning of fossil fuels to generate heat in the winter.

Air Quality in the Western Ghats

A common perception is that air pollution is lower at higher altitudes. This is based on the assumption that at higher altitudes the density of traffic / population may be lower than in the plains. The results from a ride between a town (Ujire) located at the base of the Western Ghats at an altitude of about 100 meters to another town (Chikkamagalur) at an altitude of 1000 meters did not show any significant gain in air quality with higher altitude.

The road (Charmadi ghat) is a fairly busy road starting at the base of the Deccan plateau and ending near the top of the plateau, passing through rainforests. AQI on the Charmadi ghat ranged from a minimum of 25 to a maximum of 100 near a temple close to the peak of the road.

Community  Air Quality Monitoring with Low Cost Sensors

While data from public monitoring stations is usually freely available, data from private organizations is often restricted and subscription based. Use of citizen data collected from low cost sensors can provide a better understanding of the distribution of pollution than the data alone from widely distributed public monitoring stations.

Citizens can collect air pollution data, not necessarily on a bicycle, but even on a morning walk. The collected data from affordable sensors, even though of low accuracy, has value and can be used to highlight the severity of the problem of air pollution that is not obvious from data collected at official monitoring stations alone.

The initiative by AQI.IN to create a large community of citizens who will support and maintain supplied air quality monitors is laudable. However, air quality monitoring is the responsibility of the government and measures to protect public health can be undertaken when detailed information about air pollution is freely available to all.

With better data collection, an optimal route that minimizes exposure to pollution for a commuter can improve public health. Currently, most suggested routes optimize travel time alone.  

The AQI on a road used by thousands of commuters, delivery workers, and others is often higher than the reported AQI from a monitoring station. The risk of exposure to air pollution is not limited to riders on two-wheelers alone. Although cars do have air filters and the air inside a car with the air conditioner is likely to be less polluted, commuting in a car will not necessarily reduce the risk of air pollution.

The air quality data for a town like Hassan from different organizations is often not consistent for the same time and day. At noon on February 11th 2026, IQAir reported an AQI of 89, AQI.IN reported 132 and Accuweather reported 66. Data from additional sensors can improve the accuracy of the reported AQI.

A more formal evaluation of portable air quality monitors would include calibration to verify that the results from such low cost sensors are accurate and also to run tests under similar environmental conditions.

Air quality monitoring through calibrated low-cost sensors cannot replace regulatory stations, but it can provide a clearer picture of the air citizens actually breathe.
